My research seeks to understand how forests persist in a changing world. I integrate ecology, genomics, and community-engaged research to examine how climate change influences forest resilience and how different knowledge systems contribute to the stewardship of healthy landscapes.

## Forest Resilience and Regeneration

<p align="center">
  <img src="/images/forest-resilience.jpg" width="700">
</p>

Tree regeneration depends on a rare sequence of events: seed production, germination, seedling establishment, and survival. My research examines how climate and environmental drivers shape these process by combining long-term ecological datasets with field observations. 

I am especially interested in how ecological patterns change across scales. In long-term, multi-species datasets, relationships between climate, traits, and seed production can look different when examined across all species and sites, among species within sites, or within species across environmental gradients. This scale dependence matters for understanding when broad ecological patterns reflect shared environmental responses, species traits, or local evolutionary histories.

## Landscape Genomics and Local Adaptation

<p align="center">
  <img src="/images/landscape-genomics.jpg" width="700">
</p>

Species persist under environmental change in part through adaptation. My research uses landscape genomics to understand how genetic variation is distributed across environmental gradients and what it reveals about adaptation in climate-stressed tree species.

I integrate genomic data with functional and reproductive traits, including seed production frequency and magnitude, leaf traits such as stomatal traits, leaf area, and intrinsic water-use efficiency, and seed traits such as seed size, seed number, and viability. This approach links genetic variation to traits that influence persistence, reproduction, and potential responses to climate change.

A major goal of this work is to build tools and resources for non-model forest trees. This includes transcriptome resources, tissue-specific expression data, voucher specimens, and repositories that connect SNPs, traits, and museum specimens. Natural history collections are essential to this work because they preserve the biological context needed to link genomic data to plants, places, and traits over time.

By connecting genomics, traits, and environmental gradients, this research can help inform seed sourcing, restoration, and reforestation strategies for long-lived forest species facing rapid environmental change.

## Indigenous-Led Stewardship and Community-Based Monitoring

<p align="center">
  <img src="/images/stewardship.jpg" width="700">
</p>

Healthy landscapes depend not only on ecological processes but also on long-term relationships between people and place. I work with Tribal Nations, local communities, students, and land managers to develop monitoring programs that document woodland health and support stewardship decision-making.

In pinyon–juniper woodlands of the Eastern Sierra Nevada and Great Basin, I work with Tribal Nations, students, local organizations, and land managers to monitor woodland health, cone production, regeneration, and landscape change. This work centers Tribal priorities and supports monitoring approaches that are useful for stewardship decision-making, while recognizing Indigenous leadership and data sovereignty.
